Measuring bearing

True bearing is a more accurate way of giving direction than by simply using the 16 compass points.

When we use bearing to describe relative position, we measure the angle between the two points in question from true north and give the answer in degrees.

For example, the direction from point A to point B in the Figure 1 may be described as east, but the true bearing between the two points is 85°.

Figure 2 shows the 16 compass points each with their corresponding degree value. You can see that north is equal to 0° or 360°, and SSW is equal to 202,5°.

When we work on maps, we calculate the bearing between two places from true north, but how do we calculate bearing when we are out in in the field?

If you had to measure the bearing of one landmark from another in an area such as the Ai-Ais/Richtersveld Transfrontier Park you would make use of a compass. The magnetic needle of the compass points to magnetic north as opposed to true north. The angle between true north and magnetic north is called the magnetic declination. Magnetic north is currently west of true north so the angle measured by the compass (magnetic bearing) will be greater than that measured from true north. Magnetic bearing is equal to magnetic declination plus true bearing.
