Understanding data layers in GIS

GIS maps consist of different layers of data.

  • A point is a map feature that has no length or area, such as a city on a world map or a building on a city map.
  • A line, also known as an edge, is a map feature that has length but not area, such as a river on a world map or a contour on a topographic map.
  • Nodes are the beginning or ending points of an edge or one of the three corners of a triangle.
  • Areas or polygons are features on a map that have a closed, two-dimensional shape.
  • Points, lines and polygon data are vector data.
  • Satellites generate raster data, consisting of pixels.
  • Pixels are the smallest unit in which raster data is stored and make up a regular grid of cells that are referenced in rows and columns.

When using a GIS to map data it is best to have points overlaid on top of lines overlaid on top of polygons overlaid on top of raster data, i.e. stack the layers from top to bottom as point; line; polygon and raster. Data will be hidden if it is not placed in this order. For example, if you place an urban area polygon on top of a spot height point, you will not see the spot height.
