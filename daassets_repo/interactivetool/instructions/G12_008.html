GIS Processing – statistical analysis

Statistical analysis refers to a collection of methods used to process large amounts of data and report overall trends. Statistical analysis is particularly useful when dealing with noisy data or too many datasets.

An attribute or field in a spatial dataset is exactly the same as a spreadsheet or database field. So, you can do the same types of statistical analysis and use the same functions and operations in a GIS as in a spreadsheet or database.

Statistical techniques are used to explore various aspects of spatial data or to perform various operations on it:

  • distributions, e.g. find out the frequency distribution of ages in the population.
  • classification, e.g. group temperatures into meaningful categories and colour them to make a temperature map of South Africa.
  • patterns, e.g. see whether there is a spatial pattern to vehicle thefts in Johannesburg (i.e. are they clustered anywhere?) or whether they are randomly dispersed.
  • aggregation or summaries, e.g. aggregate enumerator area data (the smallest unit in the Census) to report at the level of a municipality aggregate raster data by zone or polygon, e.g. calculate mean elevation per forest compartment.

Spatial statistics, a fundamental concept in Geography is that the closer things are to each other, the more similar they are. Various spatial statistics, such as interpolation, depend on this phenomenon.

A statistic called ‘spatial autocorrelation’ measures the degree to which things are similar in relation to their distance apart.
