Spot heights Peak heights The highest point of a hill or a mountain is called the peak. A triangular point symbol ▲ is used on maps to show the position and height of the peak of a hill or a mountain. On small-scale country maps, as shown below, the triangle usually has a black fill. The number close to the triangle is the height of the peak. When surveyors measure the heights of places, and the distances between places on the ground, each place where they take measurements is known as a spot height. Many thousands of these spot heights are measured; some of them are marked on the maps which are made using surveyors’ measurements. On a map, a spot height consists of a dot with a height measurement beside it. Hypsometric tints On small-scale relief maps of countries or continents, different colours can be used to represent land at different heights. Places close to sea level are usually shaded green. As the height of places increases, the colours used range from pale orange to brown and then to purple. The colour tints become more intense (darker) as height measurements increase. Hill shading Another way to represent relief on a map is by light and dark shading (see the sample map, above, on the right). Slopes which face towards the sun are shaded lighter. Darker shading is used to represent the shadows cast by hills and mountains. With modern digital imagery, the shadows are generated using slope information. Very effective 3D views of the landscape can be produced in this way.
