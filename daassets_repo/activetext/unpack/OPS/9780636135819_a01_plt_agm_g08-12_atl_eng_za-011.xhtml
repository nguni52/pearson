The length of night and day at places along other latitudes is influenced by Earth’s tilt, and varies according The rotation of Earth also results in differences in time at different places on Earth’s surface. It takes 24 hours for Earth to rotate once round its axis. In other words, Earth takes 24 hours to rotate through 360°; or one hour to rotate 15° (360 ÷ 24). Using the lines of longitude, we are able to work out the time at various places on Earth. Earth rotates 15° every hour. For every 15° that we go east from 0° longitude (the Greenwich Meridian), the time will be one hour later than the time at the Greenwich Meridian. If we go west by 15° from the Greenwich Meridian, the time there will be one hour earlier than at the Greenwich Meridian. World time zones Many countries are so big that they extend across more than 15° of longitude. This would mean that, in a single country, there might be towns and cities far enough away from each other as to have their own time. For very large countries, such as the United States, this is not considered to be a problem, and there is more than one time zone in the country. (See the World Time Zones map on page 67.) For smaller countries, however, for the time to be the same at any place in that country, a system of standard time zones in the world was developed. Each time zone is centred on a longitude line and extends 7½° to the west and east thereof. Every country in the world chooses the zone, or zones, they wish to use for their time. 11
